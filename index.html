ABOUT  PDCA

Developed In the 1950s,by the management consultant Dr William Edwards Deming.
A repetitive four-stage model for continous improvement in business process
management.It is a continous loop of Planning,Doing,Checking and Acting

PLAN

you identify and plan for the improvements.This involves setting objectives,identifying processes that need improvement,and developing plans to adress those areas

DO

This is the implementation phase where the planned changes are executed.It involves carrying out the plan,implementing the changes and collecting data during the process

CHECK

In this phase the result of the implemented changes are evaluated.Data collected during the 'Do' phase is analyzed to determine if the objectives were met and to identify any deviations from the plan

ACT

Based on the result from the 'Check' phase,appropriate actions are taken.If the objectives were met,the changes may be standardized.If not,further adjustments and iterations may be necessary to improve the process.

Here are some benefits of using the PDCA cycle

Continous Improvement :Helps in the continously improving processes and products
Problem-Solving :Provides a structured approach to identify and address problems.
Efficiency :Enhances efficiency by optimizing processes and reducing waste.
Risk Reduction :Minimizes risks by systematically testing and implementing changes.
Engagement :It involves employees in the improvement process, fostering a culture of teamwork and accountability.

TOP DOWN & BOTTOM UP

TOP DOWN

Top-down approach is a method or strategy that starts with a broad overview or big picture and then breaks it down into smaller, more manageable components or tasks. It involves beginning with the general concept and gradually moving towards the specifics.

Bottom-up approach is a method or strategy that starts with the individual components or details and gradually builds them up into a complete system or solution. It involves starting with the specific elements and gradually combining them to form a larger structure or concept.

BOTTOM UP

TOP DOWN

In software engineering, the top-down approach involves starting with a high-level overview of the system's
requirements and architecture.


Developers begin by identifying the main modules or components of the software system and defining their interfaces
and interactions.


After defining the structure, they progressively refine each module by breaking it down into smaller sub-modules
or functions until reaching a level where implementation details can be addressed.


This approach allows for a clear understanding of the system's architecture and facilitates parallel development by
enabling different teams to work on different modules concurrently.

BOTTOM UP

Conversely, the bottom-up approach in software engineering begins with the implementation of individual modules
or components.


Developers start by creating small, functional units of code that perform specific tasks or functions.


These modules are then incrementally integrated and tested to form larger components and eventually the entire
software system.


This approach allows for early validation of individual components and fosters iterative development, where the system evolves based on the feedback received during integration and testing.
